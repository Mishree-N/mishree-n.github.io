Current Research

Hantman Lab @ UNC Neuroscience Center

Research Statement

Currently, I work as a paid undergraduate research assistant in the Hantman Lab at UNC Medicine's Neuroscience Centerwhere we research skillful movements which contribute to the major functions of the brain, such as perception and manipulation of the world. Considering the range of possible actions and the complexity of musculoskeletal arrangements, skilled motor control is an amazing achievement of the nervous system. The myriad of these underlying operations is likely performed by a diverse set of neural circuits. By combining anatomy, physiology, and specific (genetic and temporal) manipulations, the lab aims to identify and understand the neural elements responsible for skillful motor control. Specifically, I am exploring neural mechanisms of post-stroke recovery (simulated via optogenetic interventions) in skilled motor control movement in model mice as observed via their performance on a reach and grab task.

Past Research and Publications

Independent Behavioral Biology Research: Snail Communication

As part of the IB Diploma Programme, I aimed to explore how communication via snails’ mucin trails influences trail-following behavior in the two species to determine whether pheromones influence mate and food-finding, as well as whether this behavior impacts species-specific survival success and aggregation. Specifically, I posited the following research question: How does pheromone-based communication affect trail-following in Otala lactea and Bulimulus guadalupensis as measured by the number of snails that follow a mucin trail through a Y-maze over a three-hour period?

Independent Biology Research: Contact Lenses and Infectious Disease

As part of the IB Diploma Programme, I aimed to explore the efficacy of various contact lens cleaning solutions in eradicating bacterial growth. Specifically, I posited the following research question: To what extent do various contact lens solutions (saline, multipurpose solution, and hydrogen peroxide) inhibit the number of colonies Staphylococcus epidermidis grown on silicone hydrogel contact lenses at different stages of the disinfecting technique (rub & rinse and storage), measured by colony count over 24 hours using the Rodac imprint method on TSA plates? Once complete, I submitted it to the International Young Researchers' Conference and presented it at a symposium held in Columbia University.

Biomedical Engineering Research at the University of Texas at Austin

At UT Austin's biomedical engineering lab, I aimed to utilize the novel Type 1 Secretion System (T1SS) developed for use in Gram-negative bacteria to induce the secretion of Eglin C, a pharmacologically active elastase inhibitor which has been shown to inhibit matriptase, a protease correlated with cancer progression that plays an essential role in the metastatic process of cancer cells in multiple cancer pathways. My group's aim was to achieve the large-scale secretion of Eglin C, which has become a major interest in biotherapeutics for cancer. After achieving low-scale secretion over the span of a summer, we presented our work UT Austin's poster symposium.

Health Narrative Publication

I worked with a friend undergoing treatment for a cavernous hemangioma to write this health narrative and share her story. After an immersive 7 months, I curated this paper to accurately and impactfully convey her experience based on my independent research. It addressed key aspects of healthcare, such as the importance of tact in patient care. Once complete, I submitted it to the International Young Researchers’ Conference, where I presented it virtually to a panel of judges in Tokyo, Japan.
